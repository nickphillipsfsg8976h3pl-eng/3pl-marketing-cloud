One of the hardest aspects to teaching is connecting content to purpose. I’m sure you’ve heard some variation of “Why are learning this?”, “What is the point?”, “I’ll never use this in real life” uttered by a student once or twice – usually as a defiant response to a request to complete work.

The truth is, most of us are good at explaining that there are many real-world applications of what is being studied. We can explain that there is geometry and algebra everywhere in construction and engineering. We can point to needing to know percentages or fractions to work out if sales are worth it. There’s even the obvious need for addition and subtraction.

But unless students can make the connection themselves, they’ll always consider ‘maths’ as a series of tiresome questions, completely divorced from their day-to-day reality. Many will never get that ‘lightbulb’ moment until they suddenly are thrust into the adult world of budgeting and working out personal finances and needing to use maths in their jobs. Then they might wish they paid more attention in math class on a Friday afternoon.

But what if you could make that light-bulb moment happen in our classrooms? So, how do we get our students to start paying that extra bit of attention?
