Teachers are facilitating thinking in the class

Gone are the days when the teacher stood at the front of the class and imparted knowledge. Students now have access to all the information (and distractions) they could ever want in their pockets.

Thus, the role of the educator has changed. In the eternal words of Margaret Mead,


| ‘Children must be taught how to think, not what to think.'


When students do reach for that magical, all-knowing phone, we want them to be able to question what they read, to have the tools necessary to separate fact from fiction. PSR – the way we’ve implemented it in Mathletics – asks students to play with ideas. To look at them from multiple angles. This helps learners become real-world thinkers.

The future of work

The jobs our students will be entering haven’t been invented yet. Technology is evolving rapidly, hundreds and thousands of gigabytes of information is changing and investigating every inch of human knowledge.

We can’t keep up to teach it all.

But we can help students develop nimble minds that can pick up new concepts, abilities, and skills that will help them find – not just employment – but purpose through their work.

Because it’s fun

PSR questions are fun. They’re engaging.

When done well they’re magic, transcending questions and becoming deep, rich, story-driven experiences.

But developing questions to this level can be time-consuming.
